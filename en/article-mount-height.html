Mount height and the optic's second zero

08 JANUARY 2026//BALLISTICS//HOB · FAR ZERO

Introduction

The mounting height of the optic on an AR-15 is one of those parameters that can "quietly" change how the whole setup behaves, even though the zeroing process itself looks identical: we set the point of impact at a given distance and consider the subject closed. In reality, changing the height of the sight axis over the bore (the so-called height over bore) doesn't just affect the well-known offset at close distances; it also shifts, in space, the second point where the bullet's flight path crosses the line of sight, the so-called "second zero" (far zero).

The higher the optic sits, the greater the elevation angle of the bore axis required to achieve the same zero at short distance, and that in turn changes the geometry of the entire trajectory relative to the line of sight and causes the "far" intersection to land somewhere other than the shooter intuitively assumes, based on popular schemes like 25/300 or 50/200.

We base the analysis on a common reference point: an AR-15 in .223/5.56 with a 14.5″ barrel and 55 gr FMJ ammunition. We'll compare the consequences of zeroing at 25 m and 50 m for four popular height configurations: EOTech XPS and EXPS, and an Aimpoint red dot on Scalarworks 1.93″, Unity FAST 2.26″ and GBRS Hydra 2.91″ mounts. The presented values are model values, meant to convey the trend and the scale of the changes.

What does "mount height" mean, and why does it change the second zero?

With red dot and holographic optics, what we care about isn't "how much riser there is", but the height of the aiming axis above the bore axis (HOB, height over bore). Mechanically:

  • The line of sight (LOS) is a straight line: sight → target
  • The bullet's path is a curve: it starts below the LOS, rises relative to the LOS, then falls
  • For the bullet to cross the LOS at the intended zero distance, you have to set the elevation angle of the bore axis relative to the LOS

The higher the optic (the larger the HOB), the further "below" the LOS the bullet starts at the muzzle, and the more upward the barrel has to point to hit "on point" at a short zero. As a result, the bullet stays above the LOS longer before gravity and air resistance "pull it" back down. The effect: the second zero moves further out. It's pure geometry combined with external ballistics: you change the boundary condition, so you change the angle, and the angle determines where the curve crosses the straight line a second time.

From "height above the rail" to real HOB

For the AR-15 it's commonly accepted that the distance from the bore axis to the top surface of the Picatinny rail on the upper is about 1.215″. So the approximate HOB:

FORMULA HOB ≈ (height of the optic axis above the rail) + 1.215″

You'll also often encounter the rule of thumb that the AR-15's sight plane sits about 2.6″ above the bore axis (which is where "absolute co-witness" comes from).

Configurations analyzed

  • EOTech XPS (absolute co-witness, on the rail): approx. 1.41-1.42″ above the rail
  • EOTech EXPS (lower 1/3 co-witness): approx. 1.71-1.72″
  • Aimpoint + Scalarworks 1.93: 1.93″ (49 mm)
  • Aimpoint + Unity FAST 2.26: 2.26″
  • Aimpoint + GBRS Hydra 2.91: 2.91″

How the "second zero" shifts for 25 m and 50 m zeros

Reference point: in shooting practice there's the concept of the "25/300" scheme for 5.56 carbines (near zero ~25 m, far zero ~300 m) as a training and combat shorthand. For "50/200" (often quoted in yards) the far zero is typically described as ~200-225 yd, depending on the configuration.

  • 25 m zero → far zero ~300 m for a standard height close to "absolute" (XPS)
  • 50 m zero → far zero ~210 m as the metric equivalent of the popular 50/200-225 yd descriptions

Practical interpretation

Moving from a "standard" height (around 2.6″ HOB) to 1.93″ above the rail, the far zero can shift by tens of meters. At 2.26″ and 2.91″ the change becomes "doctrinal": it starts being a different system of holds, not just cosmetics.

What this means on the target

A) The close-range offset grows

At distances in the 3-15 m range the bullet has no time to "climb" up to the LOS, so the hit will essentially be lower by the HOB. With the ~2.6″ HOB standard we're talking ~6.5-7 cm low; with the Hydra (~4.125″ HOB) we're already talking ~10.5 cm low. This is critical for:

  • precision shooting at small zones (A-zone, head box)
  • shooting "through obstacles" (ports, gaps)
  • situations where 2-4 cm of error makes the difference

B) The taller the mount, the higher the trajectory's "hump" relative to the LOS

Because you have to raise the barrel more, the bullet flies higher above the LOS in the middle phase. For a 50 m zero this often produces a comfortable "battle zero" out to ~200 m, but with very tall HOBs the maximum rise above the LOS grows, the differences between "hold center" and "hold low" at 100-150 m grow too, and it gets easier to overshoot small targets if the shooter holds "on point" without awareness of the curve.

C) Changing HOB changes not only the far zero, but the whole map of corrections

If you're moving from 1.42-1.72″ above the rail to 2.26-2.91″, it's worth building new, simple hold rules (e.g. 7 m / 15 m / 25 m / 50 m / 100 m), confirming the far zero for real (200-350 m, depending on your chosen zero), and not assuming that "50/200 will behave the same".

Practical advice: mapping your carbine after a mount change

Changing the height of the optic axis changes your close-range offset and shifts the second zero. To avoid relying on "ready-made answers", do a short mapping session after setting your zero.

1) The procedure in short

Set or confirm your zero at 25 m or 50 m, then map your hits at several distances and note the deviation relative to the dot or the crosshair.

2) The minimum set of distances

  • 5 / 10 / 15 m: offset (you hit low)
  • 25 / 50 m: zero confirmation
  • 100 m: a "midpoint" check
  • 200 / 300 m: checking the trend and verifying the second zero

3) The close-range rule

At 5-15 m the bullet practically has no time to enter the line of sight, so you hit lower by roughly the HOB. The taller the mount (1.93 / 2.26 / 2.91), the bigger the "hold up" at close range. Train with small targets to force precision and memorize the offsets.

4) How to confirm the second zero

  • 25 m zero: check a group at 300 m; with tall mounts additionally at 350-400 m
  • 50 m zero: check at 200 m; with tall mounts additionally at 250-300 m

5) The end result

Make yourself a simple "hold card" with the deviations for 5/10/15/25 (or 50)/100/200/300 m. You'll always be able to come back to it when in doubt. On top of that, practice shooting at various distances using smaller targets; you'll be forced to work with corrections, so you'll memorize them faster and they'll become intuitive.

KNOW YOUR ZERO A tall mount is a different system of holds. Map it before you trust it at distance.
