Shooting gases and your health

12 DECEMBER 2025//SHOOTER SAFETY//GSR · VENTILATION

Introduction

Shooting indoors, whether at commercial ranges or at military and police facilities, generates a range of air contaminants that pose a real, documented health hazard. While most range users are aware of the noise and the need for hearing protection, the chemical hazards and post-shot aerosols (GSR, gunshot residue) are still often underestimated or outright ignored.

The most important air contaminants produced by a gunshot are:

  • heavy metals (mainly lead, antimony, copper, zinc)
  • PM10/PM2.5 and ultrafine particles (UFP)
  • carbon monoxide (CO) and nitrogen oxides (NOx)
  • volatile organic compounds (VOCs)
  • gases and particles from primers and smokeless powder

Their concentration depends on the intensity of shooting, the firearm's design, the type of ammunition, and above all on the performance of the ventilation systems and adherence to hygiene procedures.

1. What is produced at the moment of the shot?

1.1. Heavy metals

The best-documented risk is exposure to lead (Pb), which is still present in primers, in parts of bullets, and in the micro-dust generated by the bullet's friction against the bore. Alongside lead, the aerosols contain:

  • antimony (Sb): a primer component
  • copper (Cu): the bullet jacket
  • zinc (Zn): a component of some alloys
  • barium (Ba): in GSR with certain ammunition types

These metals occur in the PM10 and PM2.5 fractions and in the ultrafine fraction (<100 nm), which is particularly dangerous because it can cross the alveolar-capillary barrier.

1.2. Gaseous compounds

  • carbon monoxide (CO): the result of incomplete powder combustion
  • nitrogen oxides (NO, NO₂): irritate mucous membranes, can cause pulmonary edema
  • sulfur dioxide (SO₂): in small amounts, depending on the powder
  • VOCs: volatile aromatic hydrocarbons and nitro compounds

During intensive shooting, CO can reach levels exceeding occupational safety limits.

1.3. Solid and ultrafine particles

Every shot generates a dust cloud containing soot, unburnt powder, micro-fragments of the bullet jacket and primer residue. The greatest risk comes from UFP (ultrafine particles) with a diameter below 0.1 micrometer: they penetrate deep into the lungs, can pass into the bloodstream, and bind heavy metals, transporting them into tissues.

2. Health effects of exposure

2.1. Lead exposure

Lead accumulates in tissues, and its effects are particularly dangerous for the nervous system. Documented effects:

  • neurological and cognitive impairment
  • hypertension and increased risk of cardiovascular events
  • hormonal disorders
  • decreased kidney function

Numerous reports have found elevated blood lead levels (BLL) in instructors teaching daily, range staff, uniformed service personnel and sport shooters using indoor facilities.

2.2. Respiratory diseases

Exposure to dust and NOx causes chronic airway inflammation, occupational asthma (documented cases), higher risk of infections and reduced lung capacity.

2.3. Short-term symptoms after a shooting session

Even a single intensive session can trigger headaches, conjunctival irritation, a metallic taste in the mouth, coughing and shortness of breath, as well as fatigue and impaired concentration.

3. The role of ventilation: the key safety element

Ventilation is the most important factor limiting exposure. Poor ventilation can raise contaminant levels as much as 20-fold.

OSHA/NIOSH standards

  • Minimum airflow velocity toward the targets: 0.3-0.4 m/s
  • An air stream from the shooter's station toward the target, without turbulence
  • HEPA filtration class H13 or higher

European standards

The EU has no single unified document, but most countries adopt values similar to NIOSH. Recommended: continuous airflow along the shooting lane, an extraction system at the target and supply air behind the shooter's back.

3.1. Air changes (ACH)

ACH (Air Changes per Hour) specifies how many times the air in the room is exchanged in an hour. The recommended minimum, based on a review of OSHA/NIOSH standards and commercial range practice: 10-12 ACH, and for high-intensity facilities 15-20 ACH.

4. Respiratory protection

When you're unsure about the ventilation, or when working in heavy smoke conditions, respiratory protection becomes necessary. Below is an overview of three equipment groups.

4.1. Premium tactical masks

SORT Respirator, Ops-Core (Gentex): a mask designed for military users. P100/HEPA-class filtration, low breathing resistance, compatibility with helmets, hearing protection and communication systems, particulate and chemical cartridges. Pros: top filtration performance, stability while running, minimal eyewear fogging. Cons: high price, limited availability in Europe.

VENTUS Respiratory TR2: a professional tactical respirator, often chosen by instructors and operators. P100 filtration, a modular filter system, very low breathing resistance, excellent compatibility with ballistic plates and helmets. Pros: one of the best breathability-to-performance ratios on the market, excellent for long indoor sessions. Cons: pricier than civilian systems, requires periodic maintenance.

4.2. Budget solutions: 3M and similar

For civilian shooters and hobbyists, 3M half masks are an excellent solution: the 7502 / 6200 with P3/P100 filters, the 6000 series, and the disposable 3M Aura FFP3. Pros: very low price, high particulate filtration performance, simple use and wide availability. Cons: no compatibility with helmets and some hearing protectors, more eyewear fogging, less stability in dynamic movement.

5. Practical recommendations for shooters and instructors

5.1. How to assess a range's ventilation

  • The air should perceptibly flow from the shooter toward the target
  • No turbulence or backflow
  • The temperature and the smell of combustion should not build up while shooting
  • The range should be able to share its ACH data or airflow measurements

5.2. When should you absolutely wear a mask?

  • When shooting in tunnels and narrow lanes
  • With many shooters on the line
  • With poor ventilation
  • When cleaning shooting lanes and stations

5.3. Which ammunition to avoid indoors?

  • Unjacketed lead ammunition (lead exposed base)
  • Ammunition with chemically aggressive primers (e.g. Barnaul, military 7.62×39)
  • Recommended: full metal jacket (FMJ) or non-toxic (NT) ammunition

5.4. Hygiene procedures after shooting

  • Wash your hands after every session. Harmful compounds and heavy metals settle on your hands; contact with food or drink before washing can lead to serious poisoning
  • Change and wash your clothing after intensive training. If your clothes "smell like the range", that's precisely the substances that have settled on them
  • Clean your firearm in a well-ventilated room; on top of the post-shot residue you're adding cleaning agents, which can also contain toxic compounds
  • Instructors and range staff: get your blood lead level tested regularly

6. Summary

Indoor shooting generates numerous contaminants (heavy metals, particulates and gases) that can lead to serious health consequences. Proper ventilation, protective masks and good hygiene are the key elements of risk reduction. Using respiratory protection, whether a tactical SORT/TR2 mask or a budget 3M, significantly reduces exposure to post-shot aerosols.

THE BOTTOM LINE The chemical risk is invisible to the naked eye, but it is real, measurable and fully documented. Approach it deliberately.
