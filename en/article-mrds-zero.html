Which zero for an MRDS on a pistol?

27 NOVEMBER 2025//BALLISTICS//PISTOL · MRDS

Mini Red Dot Sights (MRDS) on pistols are no longer surprising to anyone. In the shooting community they are now the standard, in duty, competition and defensive use alike. Despite their growing popularity, the question keeps coming up: at what distance is it best to zero a pistol equipped with an MRDS? You can find plenty of answers online, but most are based on data in the imperial system and don't dig into the pros and cons of the different options.

Below is an analysis of the four most popular MRDS zeroing distances in the metric system, based on real ballistic data.

Test parameters

  • Firearm: Glock 19 (standard 102 mm barrel length)
  • Sight: Trijicon RMR (3.25 MOA dot)
  • Ammunition: STV 9×19 mm, FMJ, 124 gr
  • Method: POA-POI offset measurements fired from full stabilization

The results show the differences between the point of aim (POA) and the point of impact (POI) at each distance, for four zero settings: 10 m, 12.5 m, 15 m and 25 m. We focus on the practically relevant distances: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 and 30 m; the values for 50 and 100 m are provided for reference only.

The data: POA-POI offsets for the four zeros

Why does the 25 m zero come out "flattest" across 5-30 m?

Zeroing at a longer distance makes the line of sight and the bullet's flight path intersect in a way that produces smaller POI fluctuations at medium and longer short-range distances. With a 25 m zero the bullet sits slightly "high" in the 10-25 m bracket, but the distribution of those deviations is more uniform, which in numbers gives a lower mean_abs (the mean absolute value of the offsets, a measure of averaged deviation) and peak-to-peak (the spread between the highest and lowest offset).

Closer zeros (10 m) cause a bigger "rise" in the 15-30 m bracket, which produces a larger max_abs (the largest absolute deviation within 5-30 m) and a larger spread.

The 25 m zero gives the best flatness across the whole 5-30 m bracket, but it has the largest (most negative) offset at 5 m: −1.14 cm. For very close hits (contact range, 2-5 m) the point of impact will sit slightly lower relative to the target. If your priority is absolute POA=POI agreement at 3-7 m, shorter zeros are better (10 m or 12.5 m).

Practical recommendations: choosing a zero for your use profile

  • Broad 5-30 m coverage, minimal POI variation → 25 m zero: the lowest mean_abs and the smallest spread, i.e. the greatest predictability across the whole bracket
  • Most contacts at 0-10 m, minimal error at 5 m → 10 m zero (or 12.5 m): a precise hit at 10 m and minimal deviation in the closest zone, at the cost of a larger spread further out
  • Precision at the middle distances, 10-25 m → 15 m zero: a very low mean deviation and a small spread across 10-30 m
  • A universal compromise for tactical/duty work → 12.5 m zero: good average results, predictable behavior at 5-30 m, a smaller max_abs than the 10 m zero

Zeroing in practice: a workshop procedure

  • A stable station and a repeatable grip: zero the MRDS with the pistol rested on a sandbag or another form of stabilization, to minimize shooter error
  • The target: a small, high-contrast point. We recommend our MRDS ZERO TARGET for a 25 m zero
  • Turn the dot's brightness down: lower brightness means a smaller visible dot. A larger dot (MOA) can obscure the target and take away the precision you need to zero
  • Controlled shots, min. 3 per group: connect the bullet holes with a marker (a triangle), draw lines from each hole to the opposite side; their intersection is the averaged point of impact. Base your corrections on it

Conclusions

The 25 m zero is the "flattest" in the sense of the smallest mean and the smallest spread of deviations across the practically important 5-30 m band. That doesn't mean it's always the best: at very close distances it produces the largest negative POI shift. Choosing a zero is a compromise between trajectory flatness over a wider bracket and a minimal offset at contact range.

It's worth noting that due to the MRDS's low position relative to the bore axis and typical pistol distances, the differences between the 10, 12.5, 15 and 25 m zeros are not as noticeable as on rifles and hover around 2 cm.

RECOMMENDATIONS Versatility at 5-30 m: 25 m zero. Priority at 0-10 m (CQB): 10 / 12.5 m. Precision at 10-25 m: 15 m.
