Suppressors: pros and cons

04 JANUARY 2026//GEAR · SAFETY//RESEARCH-BASED

Introduction

A suppressor is an engineering device that reduces the energy of the acoustic wave generated by the exit of propellant gases (the muzzle blast). Research shows it can significantly lower peak sound pressure levels at the shooter's ear and daily energy exposure, but it does not eliminate the risk of hearing damage and does not "silence" a shot to a level that is safe under all conditions.

At the same time, especially in semi-automatic and automatic firearms, a suppressor increases back pressure and can intensify gas blowback, i.e. the shooter's exposure to a mixture of gases and aerosols containing, among others, CO, metals (e.g. Pb, Cu, Zn), potentially HCN and other combustion products.

In practice this means a suppressor is usually a good idea as a health-and-safety and noise-control tool, but it demands deliberate matching to the weapon platform, ammunition and conditions (especially indoors, team work, night operations), as well as shooter-hygiene standards and exposure control.

1. What "makes the noise" in a gunshot: three sound sources

  • The muzzle blast: the dominant impulse-noise source on an unsuppressed gun; this is mainly where the suppressor works, by increasing the expansion volume and stretching out the gas release
  • The "sonic crack" (N-wave): if the bullet flies at supersonic speed, it generates a shock wave along its flight path. A suppressor cannot eliminate it, because it originates "on the bullet", not at the muzzle
  • Action noise: the bolt, case ejection, carrier movement; on semi-automatic guns it can be the perceptually dominant sound source once the muzzle blast is suppressed

The practical takeaway: "how quiet" a shot will be depends not only on the suppressor, but on the ammunition (sub/super), the platform (bolt vs semi/auto), the barrel length and the gas system.

2. The benefits of running a suppressor

2.1. Hearing protection: a real exposure reduction, but no "miracle"

NIOSH field studies showed that suppressors can lower exposure at the shooter's ear by ~17-24 dB (peak) and lower LAeq,8h by ~9-21 dB, depending on the gun and ammunition. For an instructor standing about 1 m behind the shooter, the reductions were sometimes even greater. At the same time the authors state plainly: even with a suppressor, hearing protection should still be worn, because the accumulation of impulses can still pose a significant risk.

The key safety implication: a suppressor is an engineering noise control, but it does not replace hearing protectors; it raises the safety margin and working comfort.

2.2. Lower acoustic load on the surroundings

Studies conducted in Poland indicate that a suppressor can change the directionality and spectrum of gunshot noise, which matters for modeling the environmental impact of shooting ranges (subjectively: the sound "carries less" in certain directions). A common communication problem: manufacturers quote reductions in e.g. dB(C), which is often mistakenly equated with perception in dB(A).

2.3. Better team communication and coordination

This is a secondary effect of reducing the impulse and the concussion (especially indoors): it's easier to maintain voice communication and situational awareness. Particularly important for instructors and parallel shooting.

2.4. Muzzle flash reduction

Many configurations reduce muzzle flash and light signature, which matters for training (night work) and operations. The scale depends on the ammunition and barrel length.

3. Costs and risks: where the math gets less obvious

3.1. Blowback gases and aerosols

The environmental and hygiene literature on shooting ranges has repeatedly identified CO as a significant component (poisoning risk under unfavorable ventilation), mixtures of particles and metals (including Pb) translating into measurable increases in blood lead levels, and the possibility of toxic byproducts forming (including HCN) along with other combustion products.

What does the suppressor change? On semi-automatic guns it usually increases back pressure and can intensify the amount of contaminated gases escaping through the ejection port and the bolt area, which is exactly where the shooter's face is.

THE CORE OF IT A suppressor can improve acoustic safety while simultaneously worsening inhalation and eye safety, if the platform and suppressor generate heavy blowback.

3.2. Lead exposure and shooter hygiene

A review of research on lead exposure indicates that shooting (especially at indoor facilities) is associated with elevated blood lead levels (BLL) and real health effects; the problem concerns not only staff but recreational users as well. OSHA and NIOSH guidelines describe the exposure mechanisms (inhalation/ingestion of dust), the need for monitoring, and control measures: ventilation, wet cleaning, a ban on dry sweeping, personal hygiene.

In practice: if a suppressor intensifies the ejection of contaminants through the ejection port, then contamination management (the gun, hands, clothing, the shooting station) becomes even more important.

3.3. Reliability and how the gun runs

  • Increased back pressure can raise bolt speed and affect reliability
  • Fouling and thermal load increase
  • Weight and length are added at the muzzle: ergonomics, fatigue, work in tight spaces
  • The risk of handling errors grows, e.g. overheating and burns

3.4. "Hearing safe" as a marketing myth

The NIOSH results are unambiguous: a suppressor reduces exposure, but cumulative shooting can still exceed safe doses, so hearing protectors remain the standard.

4. Barrel length and back pressure: why short barrels "punish" suppressors

A shorter barrel usually means higher uncorking pressure and a more aggressive gas impulse at the muzzle, which increases the load on the suppressor and can worsen blowback in gas-operated guns. Measurements (AR-15, M855 ammunition) show that bore pressure at bullet exit and unsuppressed noise levels rise sharply as the barrel gets shorter.

In practice: short barrel + suppressor + DI is the configuration where obnoxious blowback and fouling are observed most often. A longer barrel gives the powder more time to burn (less flash and impulse at the muzzle) and gentler working conditions for the suppressor.

5. DI vs piston

  • DI routes part of the gases directly into the carrier/BCG, which promotes fouling, and with a suppressor added it can intensify emissions through the ejection port
  • Piston systems isolate part of the gases from the receiver, which often reduces fouling of the bolt group and can limit perceived blowback, though this is not guaranteed in every design

The research and measurement perspective emphasizes that the phenomenon is highly dependent on the gun + suppressor combination, not just the ammunition.

6. Subsonic vs supersonic ammunition

6.1. The "sonic crack" (N-wave)

In supersonic flight the bullet produces a shock wave with a characteristic shape (the N-wave). For an observer "to the side" of the flight path, this sound is often more distinct than the residual muzzle sound of a suppressed gun.

6.2. Why subsonic changes the game

NIOSH research showed that reductions measured as "sound power" can be much larger with slower ammunition, because you eliminate the contribution of the N-wave, which the suppressor doesn't control. A practical note: subsonic ammunition in semi-automatic guns can be less reliable (depending on the platform and gas system), and in many applications it has other ballistic limitations. It's always a trade-off.

7. "Flow-through" suppressors and limiting gas exposure

From a safety standpoint, the key concept is back pressure. Classic suppressors, by "bottling up" the gases for longer, can increase back pressure and blowback. "Flow-through" / "low back pressure" designs aim to guide the gas stream so as to limit gases flowing back into the gun's action, route a larger share of the gases "forward" and reduce exposure in the operator's face area.

How to limit your exposure

  • Choose a lower-back-pressure suppressor where blowback is the priority
  • Adjust the gas system on platforms that allow it
  • Use dedicated blowback-reducing components, e.g. purpose-built AR-15 charging handles
  • Manage ventilation and positioning: indoor spaces, team shooting, the instructor "behind" the shooter
  • Hygiene procedures that reduce ingestion and inhalation of dust

8. Standards and safety practices

MIL-STD-1474E: the military standard for permissible noise levels and measurement requirements. NATO AEP-4875: the methodology for measuring the acoustic signature of suppressors (NIOSH discusses its scope and limitations, including that it doesn't account for the N-wave). The hardest hygiene guidance comes from industrial hygiene for shooting ranges: NIOSH (lead and noise: work organization, ventilation, cleaning, personal hygiene) and OSHA (controlling lead exposure at shooting facilities).

Hygiene recommendations

  • Under prolonged exposure to gases coming back from the suppressor, a protective mask is recommended; more in our article on the gases produced when shooting
  • After every session with a suppressor, wash your hands and face well, ideally with products that remove heavy metals
  • Avoid contact with food and drinks before washing your hands

Conclusions: when is a suppressor a good idea, and when can it hurt you?

  • A suppressor is a valuable engineering noise control (the reductions are measurable), but it does not lift the obligation to protect your hearing
  • On semi-automatic and automatic guns the acoustic gain can come with an inhalation cost (blowback), which calls for lower-back-pressure designs and hygiene discipline
  • Using a suppressor is justified, among others, indoors, at night and in team work. Using it as a "cool factor", without analyzing the platform and exposure, can be a suboptimal choice health-wise

Glossary

  • AEP-4875: NATO Allied Engineering Publication, the protocol for measuring suppressor acoustic signatures
  • MIL-STD-1474E: the standard for measuring and limiting noise of military systems
  • SPL: Sound Pressure Level
  • dB(A), dB(C): frequency weightings (perception / band energy)
  • LAeq,8h: equivalent sound level referenced to 8 h (a dose measure)
  • N-wave: the shock wave (sonic crack) of a supersonic bullet
  • DI: direct impingement, gases routed to the carrier
  • Blowback: gases and aerosols flowing back toward the shooter (bolt area / ejection port)
  • CO / HCN / PAH / Pb / BLL: carbon monoxide / hydrogen cyanide / polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons / lead / blood lead level
  • OSHA / NIOSH: US occupational health and safety institutions and standards
